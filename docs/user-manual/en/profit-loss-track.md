# Profit & Loss Track

## Access
- In the web app, open `Performance`.
- Select `Profit & Loss Track`.

Use this page in the web app when you want to manage trading as a business, not only as a P&L chart.

## What this page is for
Profit & Loss Track helps you answer five practical questions:
1. How much does it cost to run my trading operation?
2. What is my minimum monthly break-even?
3. Which tools and subscriptions are helping or hurting cash flow?
4. Am I staying inside the spending limits I planned?
5. Is the business actually profitable after expenses?

This page is not only for subscriptions. It is also for:
- broker-related costs
- market data
- software
- courses
- mentoring
- funded-account fees
- one-time business purchases

## Before you start
Have these numbers ready:
1. Your initial business capital.
2. Your average trading days per month.
3. Your average number of trades per month.
4. Your recurring tools and subscriptions.
5. Any major one-time spending you want to track.

If you do not know exact values yet, start with honest estimates. You can refine them later.

## Period filters
At the top of the page, choose:
- `Week`
- `Month`
- `Quarter`
- `Semiannual`
- `Annual`

Use them like this:
- `Week`: short control check
- `Month`: normal operating review
- `Quarter`: spending discipline and trend review
- `Semiannual`: bigger business review
- `Annual`: full-year view

Important:
- The budget is entered as a monthly budget.
- The system automatically scales that monthly budget to the period you select.

## Business setup
This is the first section you should complete.

### Trader type
What it is:
- your operating style

Why it matters:
- it changes the suggested tool stack and the recommended budget starting point

What to choose:
- the type that most closely matches how you actually trade today

### Initial business capital
What it is:
- the money you consider the starting capital of the trading business

Why it matters:
- it is used for runway and business context

What to enter:
- the real capital base you are willing to treat as business capital

Important:
- this is not treated as an expense
- do not enter your profit goal here

### Trading days / month
What it is:
- how many days you normally trade in one month

Why it matters:
- it drives the daily break-even calculation

What to enter:
- your realistic average, not your best month

### Average trades / month
What it is:
- how many trades you usually take in one month

Why it matters:
- it drives the per-trade minimum calculation

What to enter:
- your normal average

### Owner pay target / month
What it is:
- the amount you want the business to produce for you personally each month

Why it matters:
- if you include it, the page raises the business break-even target

What to enter:
- the amount you want to be able to withdraw consistently

### Include education in break-even
Turn this on if:
- you want courses, coaching, and similar learning costs to count toward the minimum profit the business must cover

Turn this off if:
- you want to review education separately from core operating needs

### Include owner pay in break-even
Turn this on if:
- you want the page to calculate the minimum result needed to cover both operating costs and your target personal draw

Turn this off if:
- you only want operating break-even without personal withdrawal pressure

## Stack & expenses
This is where you build the real cost structure of your trading business.

### Add stack item
Use this form for any cost that is not already in the suggested list or when you want full control.

#### Item name
What it is:
- the name of the tool, service, purchase, or fee

What to enter:
- a clear label such as `TradingView`, `Mentor`, `Bookmap`, `Prop fee`, or `Course`

#### Category
What it is:
- the type of business cost

Why it matters:
- categories drive budgeting, comparisons, and control alerts

Pick the category that best matches the purpose of the cost.

#### Billing cycle
What it is:
- how often the charge happens

Examples:
- weekly
- monthly
- quarterly
- semiannual
- annual
- one-time

Why it matters:
- the system converts every item into a monthly equivalent so it can calculate break-even correctly

#### Amount
What it is:
- the actual charge amount for that billing cycle

What to enter:
- the amount exactly as billed

Examples:
- if a tool costs $39 each month, enter `39`
- if a course cost $600 once, enter `600`

#### Vendor
What it is:
- the company, coach, platform, or provider charging you

Why it matters:
- vendor tracking helps you see who is consuming the most cash flow

#### Start date
What it is:
- when the expense started

Why it matters:
- it helps estimate renewals and period spend

#### End date
Use this when:
- the item stops on a known date
- a trial ends
- a program has a fixed duration

If it is ongoing, you can leave the end date empty.

#### Amortization months
This appears for one-time items.

What it means:
- instead of treating the full amount as if it belongs to one month, the system spreads it across several months

When to use it:
- courses
- large one-time purchases
- setup costs you want to spread over time

Example:
- a $600 course over 12 months becomes a $50 monthly equivalent

#### Notes
Use this for:
- plan name
- renewal notes
- package details
- anything you want to remember later

#### Active
Turn this on when:
- the cost is currently part of the business

Turn it off when:
- you want to keep the record but stop counting it as active

#### Include in break-even
Turn this on when:
- the cost should count toward the minimum profit the business must produce

Turn it off when:
- you want to track the cost, but not include it in the minimum break-even target

## Break-even cards
The cards near the top give the fastest financial read on the business.

### Monthly break-even
This is:
- the minimum monthly net trading result needed to cover the selected business costs

Use it as:
- your core monthly business floor

### Selected period break-even
This is:
- the break-even target for the filter you chose at the top

Examples:
- if you are viewing `Quarter`, this shows the quarter break-even
- if you are viewing `Week`, this shows the weekly break-even

### Daily minimum
This is:
- the average net result needed per trading day

How it is calculated:
- monthly break-even divided by your trading days per month

### Per trade minimum
This is:
- the average net result needed per trade

How it is calculated:
- monthly break-even divided by your average trades per month

### Actual net after operating expenses
This is:
- your real net result for the selected period after operating expenses

### Above / below break-even
This is:
- the difference between your actual net result and the break-even target for the selected period

How to read it:
- positive means you are above break-even
- negative means the business is still below its required minimum

## Budget vs actual
This section compares planned spending against real spending.

### Monthly budget
This is:
- the total monthly budget you saved

### Full period budget
This is:
- the full planned budget for the selected period

### Budget to date
This is:
- the part of the selected period budget that should have been used by now

Why it matters:
- it is more fair than comparing mid-month spending to a full-month budget

### Actual operating spend
This is:
- the real expense total for the selected period

### Variance to date
This is:
- budget to date minus actual spend

How to read it:
- positive is favorable
- negative means overspend

### Previous period spend
This is:
- what the same type of period cost before

Use it to see:
- whether spending is rising or falling

### Observed trading fees
This shows:
- the commissions and fees detected from trading records

Use it to keep variable trading costs visible, even when those costs are already part of trading P&L.

### Preset monthly budget
This shows:
- the suggested monthly budget based on your trader type

Use it as:
- a reference point, not a rule

## Best practices
- Update your stack as soon as a tool is added, removed, or changed.
- Use realistic trading days and trade count averages.
- Keep capital separate from expenses.
- Use `Budget to date` during the month, not only full-month totals.
- Review renewals before they hit cash flow.
- Keep education and owner pay rules consistent so your break-even stays meaningful.

## FAQ
### Should I put my trading account balance in Initial business capital?
Use the amount you truly treat as business capital. It should represent the capital base of the operation, not a dream goal.

### Should every cost be included in break-even?
No. Include costs that should count toward the minimum result the business must cover. Some users track certain items without forcing them into break-even.

### Why can the page show a budget warning before the month ends?
Because it uses `Budget to date`, which compares your spending with the portion of budget that should already be used by now.

### Why do one-time items ask for amortization months?
Because a one-time purchase can be spread over time instead of distorting one month.
